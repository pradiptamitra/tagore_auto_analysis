# Findings: Puja vs Prem pronoun-ratio evolution

Restricted analysis of the 1st/2nd-person pronoun ratio, Puja and Prem only (1026 songs).

## Headline numbers

| | rho | p | n |
|---|---|---|---|
| Combined per-song | 0.111 | 3.8e-4 | 1026 |
| Puja only | **0.165** | 3.3e-5 | 626 |
| Prem only | −0.041 | 0.41 (ns) | 400 |
| Partial (controlling for category) | 0.075 | 0.017 | 1026 |

**Pooled ratio early (<1900) → late (≥1920): 0.70 → 1.16.**
Decomposition: within-category 91%, category-mix 9%.

The mix component is slightly positive here because the late-period mix is 67% Prem / 33% Puja vs early 57% Puja / 43% Prem — and Prem has a higher baseline ratio, so shifting mix toward Prem nudges the combined ratio up too. But the dominant force (~91%) is **Puja-internal**: Puja's pooled ratio crosses parity around 1905 (0.50 → 0.81 → 1.29) and stays above 1.0 thereafter, while Prem hovers in the 1.1–2.9 range with no temporal trend.

## Two narratives that fall out cleanly

1. **Prem was always 1st-person.** Love songs were introspective from the start — the speaker's heart, longing, eyes — and stayed that way for 60 years.
2. **Puja became more like Prem.** Devotional songs started overwhelmingly addressing God in 2nd person ("তুমি/তব"), then shifted to a balanced/1st-leaning mode where the worshipper's own self enters the frame. The shift began around 1900–1905 and was complete by ~1915.
